RisQ Weather Intelligence

Weather risk dashboard, assistant workflow, and decision UX

A live U.S. weather risk intelligence dashboard combining a Leaflet map, forecast stress layers, NOAA/NWS alerts, ranked regions, local detail panels, comparison tools, and a weather-aware assistant called SkyScout. The system is designed to be useful without pretending to know things it cannot know: if provider data fails or a user asks for traffic, staffing, package tracking, road closure, or emergency-response information, the assistant names the missing data instead of fabricating confidence.

  • Built around public weather sources including Open-Meteo, NOAA/NWS, met.no fallback, geocoding, and map layers.
  • Structured assistant context from selected region, active map layer, visible points, alerts, provider status, and conversation state.
  • Demonstrates product judgment across data quality, AI guardrails, operational decision-making, and user trust.

Personal passions & interests

The curiosity behind the work.

Space & science

My fascination with the cosmos goes beyond professional atmospheric science. I follow space exploration, astronomy, launch systems, and climate observations because they keep me thinking at systems scale. That perspective shapes how I approach complex work: zoom out far enough to understand the whole system, then zoom back in to make the next decision precise.

Science fiction and future systems

I enjoy science fiction because the best stories are really about tradeoffs: technology and humanity, progress and risk, ambition and responsibility. Films like Interstellar, Ex Machina, and Blade Runner 2049 tend to spark the same questions I ask in work: what does the system optimize for, who trusts it, and what happens when assumptions break?

Sports and team dynamics

Soccer, basketball, cricket, and table tennis all scratch a different part of my brain. They are strategy, execution, feedback, pressure, and trust in motion. Sports have taught me a lot about leadership: the best teams are not the ones with the loudest direction; they are the ones that understand spacing, timing, and how to adjust together.

Creative expression

Video editing has helped me think more clearly about storytelling, pacing, and visual communication, skills that surprisingly translate into better presentations and data narratives.
